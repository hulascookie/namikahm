Final Essay for MCC219 Language, Politics and the Media

 

Perspectives such as Semiotics, Speech Act Theory, Gender Studies are helpful in analyzing discourse, power and knowledge in media. The main purpose of this essay is to analyze the film Beautiful Boy (2018) through the lens of deconstruction by the Algerian-born French philosopher Jacque Derrida (1930-2004). In doing so, it clarifies that the film itself challenges the binary opposition of the sober and the addicted by exploring the intimate father-son relationship of the main characters.

Abstract:

Drug usage and overdose pose significant challenges globally, with approximately 5% of the world's population engaging in drug use and 585,000 deaths attributed to drug overdose in 2017 alone. Factors such as the COVID-19 pandemic and mass media influence further accelerate drug consumption trends. Films and series, like "Breaking Bad" and "Beautiful Boy," play a role in shaping perceptions of drug use. "Beautiful Boy" (2018) offers a nuanced portrayal of addiction, challenging societal stereotypes. This paper employs Jacques Derrida's deconstruction theory to analyze the film's depiction of the binary opposition between the sober and the addicted. Derrida's concept of différance highlights the fluidity of binary distinctions, revealing their interdependence. By privileging the traditionally underprivileged term, the film disrupts conventional hierarchies, inviting viewers to reconsider their preconceptions. Through this lens, the essay explores how the film's authenticity contrasts with traditional media glorification of drug use, prompting viewers to reconsider societal attitudes toward addiction. Ultimately, this study illuminates the transformative potential of cinematic narratives in challenging normative standards and fostering understanding, resulting from critical examination.
